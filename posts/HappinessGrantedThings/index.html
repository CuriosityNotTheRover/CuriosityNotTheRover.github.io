Happiness and Granted Things

2023-12-20 - Written by: Filippo

I feel cold and surrounded by the dark. I can’t imagine what my grandmother feels. It terrifies me.

This picture is dear to my heart. It was taken on my grandmother’s birthday before blowing the candles. I was elsewhere. In the photo, she smiles. The face lit up. The joy of a grandmother hits differently. When I see her smile, it is heartwarming. Yet the picture hides. It hides a profound story. It is the story of a person with dementia.

Living with Dementia

A diagnosis that completely changes the life of a person. I am even closer to my nana now. I think she needs the support of the people she trusts now. I only have unconditional love for her.

Being so close to my nana, I wonder what living with dementia feels like. I ask myself questions to try to clarify my thoughts. Is she fighting a disease? My grandfather fought with cancer. I saw him before he passed. He fought with every energy to remain near what he built and his family. His mentality was the one of a fighter. My nana doesn’t seem to be fighting. She doesn’t even perceive her condition. Her opponent? She reminds me to write it down, or I’ll forget what she told me. Other times, she tries to remember what day it is by circling the number on the calendar she has on the wall. She keeps forgetting what’s the current month. It’s an empty arena. No fight is in sight.

Bothering to reason logically hurts. It is not emotionally relatable. I cannot feel what forgetfulness feels like. The dark surrounds me. Not able to shine light on the unknown. Not understanding. Cold soon comes when I realize I will lose her because of dementia.

Hope

At times, I see her happy and unbothered. Present enjoying the moment. Other times, her face shows the signs of time. The eyes barely want to focus on something. She seems absorbed by something worrying. It’s not easy to understand what’s going on inside her.

Although the picture gives me hope. It portrays a life worth living. It keeps dignity. The mere act of living requires courage from my perspective. I can’t imagine the struggles of someone living with dementia. My grandmother is enjoying her life. As long as she’s happy, I stay strong.

Fear

An interesting pattern appears when you visit nursing homes. People with mileage enjoy people’s company. They smile a lot to people. They care little about things. Sometimes, not even food is that appealing. Yet they always smile at nurses or family members visiting. Grannies with dementia seem to have the biggest smiles. I believe it is because they don’t remember the last time the family visited.

The time confusion seems meaningless when they see relatives. The joy is pervasive, and they genuinely show the biggest smiles.

That’s a sand castle. Frail foundations cannot sustain this type of happiness. Elders smile because they recognize their loved ones. Yet the memory is fading away. They can’t help it. They would hold faces in their hearts if they could. Sadly, seniors have no power. Families have no control, either. No one can interfere with this depressing fading. My worst fear is nana not recognizing me anymore.

Happiness and Dementia

I wonder if to be happy, memory is needed. Otherwise, happiness would be a mere reaction to a stimulus. Exploring this topic will be thrilling. I think fascinating results are waiting.
